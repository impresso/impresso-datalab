SRG SSR operates as Switzerland's federal public broadcasting umbrella organization with collections spanning from the 1920s to present, encompassing four language regions (German, French, Italian, Romansh) plus Swiss Radio International. The lacquer disc collection (1932-1955) contains approximately 100,000 recordings, with French-speaking regions contributing the largest portion (80,000), German-speaking areas 17,000, and Italian/Romansh regions significantly smaller amounts. Content reflects wartime propaganda functions, featuring reportages on national institutions, sports, and "spiritual defense" programming emphasizing Swiss German over standard German. The collection includes substantial wartime material, though systematic news recording was limited due to external government control until the 1960s-70s editorial independence transition. Digital production workflows began in the 1990s for automated music programming and around 2000 for political/cultural content, enabling systematic archival selection through formal editorial-archivist collaboration. Mass digitization achieved approximately one million digitized carriers by the 2010s, though cataloging remains incomplete. Regional variations exist in archival practices, with German-speaking areas maintaining more systematic local news recording from 1978 onward, while French and Italian regions relied more on correspondent networks. The collections demonstrate Switzerland's federalist structure through decentralized content creation but centralized preservation coordination, reflecting both linguistic diversity and national unity imperatives throughout the country's broadcasting history.

Memoriav functions as the coordinating institution for SRG SSR's audiovisual heritage preservation, operating independently from the broadcaster while facilitating systematic digitization and archival standards across the multilingual federation. Beginning with emergency "urgent measures" digitization in 1992-93 to preserve deteriorating lacquer discs, Memoriav established technical protocols and selection criteria through collaborative negotiations between archivists and editorial staff. The organization coordinated the transfer of approximately one million carriers to digital format, implementing systematic approaches that prioritized content based on cultural significance over production needs. Since the 2016 broadcasting law mandating SRG SSR's self-responsibility for archival preservation, Memoriav has shifted focus to supporting private and community radio digitization projects, while maintaining its role as a national portal coordinator. The institution exemplifies Switzerland's federalist approach to cultural preservation, balancing regional autonomy with national coordination through collaborative decision-making processes that respect linguistic and cultural diversity while ensuring comprehensive audiovisual heritage documentation.

## Timeline

### 1920s: First recordings

Beginning of radio in Switzerland coincided with the first commercial recording machines becoming available from Germany.

### 1931: Nationalization

Nationalization of private radio initiatives under SRG umbrella organization established the foundation for systematic broadcasting coordination.

### 1932-1955: Lacquer Disc Era

First systematic recordings begin as lacquer disc collection period commenced, marking the transition from ad-hoc to systematic recording practices and establishing the Production Archive Era focused on recording for reuse only without heritage obligations.
Collection reached approximately 100,000 recordings distributed across language regions, with French-speaking areas contributing roughly 80,000 recordings, German-speaking regions 17,000, and Italian/Romansh regions around 5,000. This period solidified the Production Archive Era where materials were preserved solely for operational reuse.

### 1939-1945: World War II and spiritual defense

Radio transformed into a "spiritual defense" propaganda medium emphasizing Swiss German over standard German, featuring reportages and weekly war commentary programs like René Payot's French-speaking broadcasts, with recordings focusing on national institutions and anti-fascist messaging.

### 1945: Echo der Zeit & Mirror du Monde/Temps

"Echo der Zeit" program began, establishing what would become the flagship German-language program continuing to the present day.

### 1950-2000: Magnetic Tape Dominance Era

Technology transition caused a significant decrease in recorded material as the shift away from lacquer disc technology toward magnetic tape began, initiating the Magnetic Tape Dominance Era that would last until 2000.

### 1960s-1970s: Editorial independence

Editorial independence transition from government news agencies occurred as radio stations gained control over news content, ending external federal press agency control and establishing broadcaster autonomy.

### 1964: National exhibition in Lausanne

National exhibition in Lausanne catalyzed editorial autonomy, enabling radio to begin producing its own news programs independent of government sources.

### 1970s: Editorial self-empowerment

Government-issued news programs ("RTS or SDR") were discontinued, achieving complete editorial self-empowerment for Swiss broadcasting institutions.

### 1978: Begin systematic recordings in the German regions

German-speaking regions began systematic regional news recording using cassette-based recording of local FM programming, introducing sub-regional news splits while French/Italian regions maintained correspondent-based coverage systems.

### 1980s: Shift towards electronic archiving

Heritage Recognition Era began as cultural value acknowledgment emerged, accompanied by the arrival of a professional archival staff generation and the introduction of electronic archiving systems with first computers, coinciding with private radio emergence leading to 1983 developments.

### 1983: Begin private radio broadcasting

Private radio broadcasting officially began, creating a major shift in the Swiss radio landscape and diversifying the archival preservation challenge.

### Late 1980s: Radio-TV law discussion

First discussions about radio-television law commenced alongside growing cultural heritage awareness among historians and cultural professionals, setting the stage for systematic preservation approaches.

### 1990s: Shift towards systematic archiving

Government-financed heritage preservation actions began creating soft pressure for systematic archiving practices, marking the transition from Production Archive to Heritage Recognition approaches.

### 1991: Radio-TV law in place

First radio-television law was enacted, providing legal framework for broadcasting obligations including nascent preservation requirements.

### 1992-1993: Begin Systematic Digitization

Systematic Digitization Era began with "urgent measures" digitization project launching emergency preservation of deteriorating lacquer discs. Memoriav's systematic digitization approach was established with initial goals of 20% preservation that ultimately achieved 90-95% success rates.

### 1993: Digital music

Digital music production systems were implemented, beginning automated music programming workflows that would transform broadcast operations.

### 2000: Shift towards digital preservation

Full digital production systems for political/cultural content were implemented, ending the Magnetic Tape Era (1950-2000) and beginning systematic mass digitization with server storage capabilities.

### 2000s: Mass digitization projects expands

Mass digitization projects expanded across all carriers as SRG SSR began independent mass digitization efforts, supplemented by lottery fund financing for regional content preservation.

### 2007-2014: Peak of coordinated preservation efforts

Major digitization phase continued with approximately one million carriers digitized to servers, representing the peak period of coordinated preservation efforts.

### 2016: Begin Independent Responsibility Era

Independent Responsibility Era began with new broadcasting law enacting SRG SSR mandates to preserve and provide public access. Memoriav's role shifted from SRG SSR coordination to private radio support, establishing legal preservation obligations.

### 2016-Present: Ongoing portal integration and API development

Ongoing portal integration and API development continues as SRG SSR assumes full archival responsibility while Memoriav focuses on private and community radio projects, implementing centralized metadata coordination across language regions.

## Caveats and notes

Expect Massive Regional Imbalances: French-speaking collections outnumber German-speaking materials by nearly 5:1 in historical periods, creating potential research distortions about linguistic representation in Swiss broadcasting history.

Fragment-Based Research Strategy: Pre-1970s materials consist primarily of program segments rather than complete broadcasts, requiring researchers to adjust expectations and methodologies for incomplete source analysis.

Multi-Portal Navigation Required: Researchers must access multiple separate portals for different language regions, with no guarantee of cross-referencing capabilities or unified search functionality.
Understand Production Archive Legacy: Historical preservation decisions prioritized operational reuse over historical value, meaning culturally significant content may be absent while technically useful materials were retained.

Legal Framework Complexity: Post-2016 legal preservation obligations create different access conditions for recent versus historical materials, with varying restrictions across content types and time periods.
Cataloging Incompleteness Awareness: While materials may be digitized, metadata completeness varies dramatically, potentially making discovery difficult even for preserved content.
Selection Bias Recognition: Regional approaches to digitization prioritization differed significantly (thematic vs. systematic), creating uneven representation that may not reflect actual broadcasting patterns.
Technical Workflow Understanding: Digital-era materials exist in production systems with timestamp-based organization rather than traditional archival description, requiring different search strategies.
Institutional Contact Strategy: Direct communication with knowledgeable archivists remains essential for understanding collection contexts, as tacit knowledge about preservation decisions and content characteristics may not be documented in accessible metadata.
Timeline Sensitivity: Research questions must account for the dramatic differences in preservation practices and completeness across different historical periods, from fragmentary 1930s-1950s materials to comprehensive post-2000 coverage.

## Primary Digital Collections

### Lacquer Disc Collection (1932-1955)

Approximately 100,000 digitized recordings with severe regional imbalances - French-speaking regions contribute 80,000 recordings, German-speaking areas 17,000, Italian/Romansh regions only 5,000. Content focuses on reportages, national institutions, wartime propaganda, and "spiritual defense" programming rather than systematic broadcast documentation. Access: Not explicitly stated in interview.

### Mass Digitization Archive (1990s-Present)

Approximately one million digitized carriers accessible through SRG SSR's federated portal system, though cataloging remains incomplete. Materials span analog-to-digital transition periods with varying metadata quality and searchability limitations.
Access via individual SRG unit portals (see Links below).

### Regional News Collections (1978-Present)

Systematic German-speaking regional news recordings on cassette format, fully digitized but not replicated in French/Italian regions. Provides comprehensive local news coverage for German-speaking Switzerland with significant gaps elsewhere. Access: Not explicitly stated in interview.

### Program Documentation

#### Program Logs & Schedules

Digitized program rosters and scheduling documents, partially digitised providing researchers with broadcast planning documentation to contextualize preserved audio materials and identify preservation gaps. Access: Availability unclear

#### Manuscripts & Production Notes

Written materials including speaker announcements and program documentation, though systematic preservation varies by period and region. Access: Not explicitly stated in interview.

#### Technical & Metadata Resources

SRG SSR Centralized API: Developer-accessible programmable interface aggregating metadata from all language regions, requiring technical expertise but providing comprehensive search capabilities across federated collections. Individual SRG units maintain separate portals for user access.
Access via https://developer.srgssr.ch/
Memoriav Portal (Memobase): National coordination platform linking digitized materials from public and private radio collections, including community and pirate radio projects beyond SRG SSR coverage.
Access via https://memobase.ch/
Secondary & Contextual Resources:
Feminist & Community Radio Collections: Specialized digitization projects documenting women's programming, pirate radio history, and minority community broadcasts from 1970s-1980s, accessible through Memoriav projects with thematic organization.
Access: Available through Memoriav/Memobase platform. https://memobase.ch/
Music & Cultural Programming: Classical music recordings and light entertainment materials from wartime period, including unique Swiss-produced popular music arrangements created to compete with German broadcasting during WWII.
Access: Available through Memoriav/Memobase platform. https://memobase.ch/
Historical Documentation: Newspaper clippings, program preview publications, and critical reviews from various studios, providing contextual information about reception and cultural impact of preserved broadcasts.
Access: Available through Memoriav/Memobase platform. https://memobase.ch/
